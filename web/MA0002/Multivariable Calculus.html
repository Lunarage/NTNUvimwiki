Continuity

A function \(f(x,y)\) is continuous at \((x_0, y_0)\) if the following hold:

  1. \(f(x,y)\) is defined at \((x_0, y_0)\)
  2. \(\lim_{(x,y) \to (x_0,y_0)} f(x,y)\) exists.
  3. \(\lim_{(x,y) \to (x_0,y_0)} f(x,y) = (x_0, y_0)\)

Partial Derivatives

Definition

If \(f\) is a function of two independent variables \(x\) and \(y\), the partial derivative of \(f\) with respect to \(x\) is defined by

\[ \frac{\partial f(x,y)}{\partial x} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h,y) - f(x,y)}{h} \]

The partial derivative of \(f\) withe respect to \(y\) is defined by

\[ \frac{\partial f(x,y)}{\partial y} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x,y+h) - f(x,y)}{h} \]

Notation

\(f_x(x,y) = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\)

\(f_{yx} = \frac{\partial^2f}{\partial x \partial y} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\right)\)

Computation

To compute \(\frac{\partial f(x,y)}{\partial x}\) we treat \(y\) as a constant and differentiate \(f\) with respect to \(x\).

Disks are two-dimensional analogues to intervals

An open disk with radius \(r\) centered at \((x_0,y_0) \in \mathbb{R}^2\) is the set

\[ B_r(x_0,y_0) = \left\{ (x,y) \in \mathbb{R}^2 : \sqrt{(x-x_0)^2-(y-y_0)^2} < r \right\} \]

A closed disk with radius \(r\) centered at \((x_0,y_0) \in \mathbb{R}^2\) is the set

\[ \overline{B}_r(x_0,y_0) = \left\{ (x,y) \in \mathbb{R}^2 : \sqrt{(x-x_0)^2-(y-y_0)^2} \leq r \right\} \]

The Mixed-Derivative Theorem

If \(f(x,y)\) and its partial derivatives \(f_x,f_y,f_{xy},f_{yx}\) are continuous on an open disk centered at the point \((x_0,y_0)\), then

\[ f_{xy}(x_0,y_0) = f_{yx}(x_0,y_0) \]

Tangent Planes, Differentiability and Linearization

Functions of Two Variables

Tangent Planes

If the tangent plane to the surface \(z = f(x,y)\) at the point \((x_0, y_0, z_0)\) exists, then the tangent plane has the equation

\[ z - z_0 = \frac{\partial f(x_0,y_0)}{\partial x}(x-x_0) + \frac{\partial f(x_0,y_0)}{\partial y}(y-y_0) \]
Basically 2d equivalent of a tangent line.

Differentiability

Suppose that \(f(x,y)\) is a function of two independent variables and that both \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\) and \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\) are defined throughout an open disk containing \((x_0,y_0)\).

Some circle of inputs where the function works.

Set

\[ L(x,y) = f(x_0,y_0) + \frac{\partial f(x_0,y_0)}{\partial x}(x-x_0) + \frac{\partial f(x_0,y_0)}{\partial y}(y-y_0) \]
Linearize the function (tangent plane).

Then \(f(x,y)\) is differentiable at \((x_0,y_0)\) if

\[ \lim_{(x,y) \to (x_0,y_0)} \left| \frac{f(x,y) - L(x,y)}{\sqrt{(x-x_0)^2-(y-y_0)^2}} \right| = 0 \]
Difference between the actual function and the linearized version divided by the distance from point of linearization. The function is differentiable if the linearization and the function agrees at given point.

Furthermore, if \(f(x,y)\) is differentiable at \((x_0,y_0)\), then \(z = L(x,y)\) defines the tangent plane to the graph of \(f\) at \((x_0,y_0,f(x_0,y_0))\). We say that \(f(x,y)\) is differentiable if it is differentiable at every point of its domain.

Theorem

If \(f(x,y)\) is differentiable at \((x_0,y_0)\), then \(f\) is continuous at \((x_0,y_0)\).

Sufficient Condition For Differentiability

Suppose \(f(x,y)\) is defined on an open disk centered at \((x_0,y_0)\) and the partial derivatives \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\) and \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\) are continuous on an open disk centered at \((x_0,y_0)\). Then \(f(x,y)\) is differentiable at \((x_0,y_0)\).

Linearization

Suppose that \(f(x,y)\) is differentiable at \((x_0,y_0)\). The linearization of \(f(x,y)\) at \((x_0,y_0)\) is the function

\[ L(x,y) = f(x_0,y_0) + \frac{\partial f(x_0,y_0)}{\partial x}(x-x_0) + \frac{\partial f(x_0,y_0)}{\partial y}(y-y_0) \]

The approximation

\[ f(x,y) \approx L(x,y) \]

is the standard linear approximation, or the tangent plane approximation, of \(f(x,y)\) at \((x_0,y_0)\).

Vector-Valued Functions

Vector-valued functions \(\vec{f}:\mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m\)

\[ \vec{f}(x_1,x_2,\dots,x_n) = \begin{bmatrix} f_1(x_1,x_2,\dots,x_n) \\ f_2(x_1,x_2,\dots,x_n) \\ \vdots \\ f_m(x_1,x_2,\dots,x_n) \end{bmatrix} \]

where \(f_i : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}, i = 1,2,\dots,n\), are real-valued functions of \(n\) independent variables, then the Jacobi matrix is an \(m \times n\) matrix of the form

\[ D\vec{f} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_1} & \ldots & \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_n} \\ \vdots & & \vdots \\ \frac{\partial f_m}{\partial x_1} & \ldots & \frac{\partial f_m}{\partial x_n} \end{bmatrix} \]

The linearization of \(\vec{f}\) about the point \((x_1^*, x_2^*, \dots, x_n^*)\) is then

\[ L(x_1^*, \dots, x_n^*) = \begin{bmatrix} f_1(x_1^*,\dots,x_n^*) \\ f_2(x_1^*,\dots,x_n^*) \\ \vdots \\ f_m(x_1^*,\dots,x_n^*) \end{bmatrix} + D\vec{f}(x_1^*, \dots, x_n^*) \begin{bmatrix} x_1 - x_1^* \\ \vdots \\ x_n - x_n^* \end{bmatrix} \]

More About Derivatives

The Chain Rule for Functions of Two Variables

If \(w = f(x,y)\) is differentiable and \(x\) and \(y\) are differentiable functions of \(t\), then \(w\) is a differentiable function for \(t\) and

\[ \frac{dw}{dt} = \frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial w}{\partial y}\frac{dy}{dy} \]

Implicit Differentiation

If a function \(F(x,y)\) is differentiable, with \(x = f(x)\) and \(y = g(x)\), and \(F(x,y) = 0\) defines \(y\) implicitly as a differentiable function of \(x\), Then, at any point where \(F_y \neq 0\),

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{F_x}{F_y} \]

Directional Derivatives and Gradient Vectors

Assume that \(z = f(x,y)\) is a function of two independent variables and that \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\) and \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\) exist. Then the vector

\[ \nabla f(x,y) = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial f(x,y)}{\partial x} \\ \frac{\partial f(x,y)}{\partial y} \end{bmatrix} \]

is called the gradient of \(f\) at \((x,y)\).

The directional derivative of \(f(x,y)\) at \((x_0,y_0)\) in the direction of the unit vector \(\vec{u}=\begin{bmatrix}u_1 \\ u_2\end{bmatrix}\) is

\[ D_{\vec{u}}f(x_0,y_0) = (\nabla f(x_0,y_0)) \cdot \vec{u} \]
